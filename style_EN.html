STYLE & AESTHETICS

The visual identity that defined an era

Peaky Fashion

The Peaky Blinders' three-piece suits have become iconic in popular culture. Each outfit consists of fitted trousers, a high-buttoning waistcoat, and a double-breasted or straight jacket, all in thick wool fabrics in dark shades: charcoal gray, navy blue, deep brown. The cuts are impeccable, perfectly hugging the male silhouette. White or cream shirts, thin ties, and pocket watch chains complete the ensemble. This formal elegance deliberately contrasts with the violence of their activities.

The flat cap, the gang's distinctive element, deserves particular analysis. Popular myth claims the Peaky Blinders sewed razor blades into the visor to attack their enemies. Historically, this is largely a legend: disposable razor blades didn't exist yet during the gang's original era. In the series, this image is preserved for its dramatic visual impact. The cap becomes a symbol of status, belonging, and threat. Each member wears theirs with pride, tilted with precision.

The 1920s dress style reflects a transitional period. After World War I, men's fashion becomes more sober and functional. The Shelbys adopt this style but refine it, making it sharper. Their long wool coats, scarves, leather gloves all evoke a controlled and dangerous masculinity. Polished boots replace fine shoes when action demands it. Every sartorial detail communicates power and intention.

The influence on contemporary fashion is undeniable. Since the series aired, three-piece suits have experienced a resurgence in popularity. Flat caps are sold in trendy boutiques worldwide. Fashion brands have created collections inspired by the Peaky Blinders. Online tutorials teach how to reproduce the look: invest in well-cut suits, favor natural fabrics, accessorize sparingly, wear confidence as the ultimate accessory. Peaky style transcends the era to become timeless.

Cinematography

Peaky Blinders' art direction creates an immediately recognizable visual universe. The sets combine historical authenticity with dramatic stylization. Birmingham's streets are reconstructed with meticulous attention to detail: irregular cobblestones, weathered bricks, period posters. Yet everything is slightly amplified, darker, more atmospheric than reality. Interiors oscillate between the squalid poverty of slums and the icy opulence of aristocratic mansions. This visual duality reflects the Shelbys' ascension.

Color choices and filters define the series' identity. A desaturated palette dominates: steel gray, cold blues, earthy browns. The rare color explosions - the red of blood, the gold of jewelry, the emerald green of a dress - strike with all the more force. Cinematic grain adds an almost tactile texture to the image. Lighting favors strong contrasts, deep shadows, creating an aesthetic close to film noir. These choices transform each shot into an expressionist painting.

Iconic camera techniques include dramatic slow-motion during key moments, fluid tracking shots following characters through streets, and intense close-ups capturing every micro-expression. The camera often adopts a subjective point of view, plunging us into Tommy's perspective. Movements are calculated, never gratuitous. Framing frequently uses symmetry to suggest order and control, or conversely unbalanced compositions to evoke imminent chaos.

The most iconic shots remain etched in collective memory: Tommy walking in slow motion through Birmingham's foggy streets, cigarette on his lips; the family gathered around the council table, lit by harsh light; shootouts choreographed like deadly ballets; close-ups on Tommy's piercing blue eyes. Compared to other period series, Peaky Blinders rejects documentary realism for an almost mythological approach, transforming history into visual legend.

The Soundtrack

The anachronistic use of modern music constitutes a bold choice that defines Peaky Blinders. Rather than using exclusively period compositions, the series integrates rock, modern blues, and even hip-hop. This temporal disconnect creates contemporary energy that makes the story more accessible and visceral. The opening credits with Nick Cave's Red Right Hand immediately establish the tone: dark, menacing, hypnotic. This approach suggests that the themes - power, violence, ambition - are timeless.

Artists featured in the soundtrack form a pantheon of alternative rock and blues. Nick Cave and the Bad Seeds dominate with several tracks. Arctic Monkeys bring their raw energy. We also find PJ Harvey, The White Stripes, Jack White, Tom Waits, Anna Calvi, and dozens of others. Each track is selected for its emotional adequacy to the scene, creating powerful resonance. The music doesn't comment on the action, it amplifies it, transforming it into a total sensory experience.

The impact of musical choices is measured at several levels. Dramatically, modern music creates instant emotional bridges with contemporary audiences. Action scenes acquire heightened cinematic intensity. Contemplative moments gain poetic depth. Culturally, the series introduced a new generation to alternative artists, increasing their visibility. Several tracks experienced renewed popularity after their use in the series. The soundtrack has become cult independently of the series itself.

A complete playlist reveals the sonic progression through the seasons. Season 1 establishes the foundations with Nick Cave, The White Stripes, and Arctic Monkeys. Season 2 introduces darker sonorities with Anna Calvi and PJ Harvey. Subsequent seasons explore more varied territories, including melancholic folk, industrial rock, and even original orchestral compositions. This musical choice works because it refuses historical pastiche to capture the emotional spirit of the era rather than its sonic surface.

Visual Symbolism

Horses constantly appear as a multidimensional symbol. They first represent the Shelbys' original business, anchoring the gang in gypsy culture. More deeply, they symbolize the freedom Tommy desperately seeks but cannot attain. Scenes where Tommy rides suggest moments of temporary liberation. When a horse dies or suffers, it often foreshadows misfortune for the family. The animal's grace and power reflect the Shelbys' own aspirations.

Cigarette smoke becomes an almost permanent visual signature. Tommy smokes constantly, each puff punctuating his reflection or tension. Visually, smoke creates mysterious veils, softens contours, adds texture to the image. Symbolically, it represents the screen Tommy places between himself and the world, his way of masking his emotions. Smoke also evokes omnipresent industrialization, Birmingham's factories, the modernity devouring the old world. Each cigarette is a small act of conscious self-destruction.

Pocket watches measure more than time. Tommy constantly consults his, obsessed with precision and temporal control. Giving or receiving a watch marks ritual moments of initiation or passage. The ticking becomes a metaphor for time running out, mortality, the permanent urgency that drives Tommy. In several key scenes, the close-up on the watch creates unbearable tension. Time is the enemy that even Tommy Shelby cannot defeat, only manage.

Whiskey and alcohol punctuate every significant interaction. Pouring a drink is a ritual of negotiation, celebration, commemoration. The type of alcohol communicates status: Irish whiskey for origins, champagne for social ascension. Characters drink to forget, to celebrate, to seal agreements. Latent alcoholism runs through the entire family, a symptom of their traumas. Weapons also carry ritual significance: Tommy's revolver becomes an extension of his will, each chosen weapon communicates the intention and style of its wielder.
