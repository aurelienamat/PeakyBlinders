THE SHELBY UNIVERSE

From Small Heath to London salons

Birmingham 1920

Birmingham in the 1920s was the industrial heart of England, a city of smoke, red bricks, and striking contrasts. Small Heath, the working-class neighborhood where the Shelbys operate, represented the brutal reality of post-war life: factories as far as the eye could see, cobblestone streets greasy with soot, and a population marked by poverty and the traumas of world conflict. It's in this oppressive setting that the Shelby family builds their empire.

Post-war England goes through a period of profound upheaval. Thousands of soldiers return from the front, traumatized and disillusioned, to a country struggling to offer them a future. Unemployment explodes, social tensions intensify, and veterans like the Shelbys use their military skills in organized crime. This era also sees the rise of extreme political movements, from communism to fascism, creating fertile ground for gangster ambitions.

Small Heath and its adjacent neighborhoods form a fascinating microcosm. The Garrison Pub serves as the unofficial headquarters, narrow streets facilitate territorial control, and metalworking factories provide both cover jobs and racketeering opportunities. Horse markets, clandestine racetracks, and smoke-filled back rooms constitute the infrastructure of organized crime. Every street corner has its history, every pub its loyalties.

The true history of the Peaky Blinders dates back to the 1890s-1910s. These street gangs took their name from the peaked caps they wore, although the myth of razor blades sewn into them is largely exaggerated. They controlled illegal betting and protection rackets. The series takes considerable creative liberties but captures the essence of this era when urban gangs wielded real power in British working-class neighborhoods.

The Shelby Family

The Shelby family tree reveals a complex dynasty. At the top, Arthur Shelby Sr., the absent and unreliable father. His children: Arthur Jr., the unstable and violent older brother; Tommy, the strategist; John, the impulsive charmer; Ada, the idealistic rebel; and Finn, the innocent youngest. Polly Gray, the matriarchal aunt, acts as the true emotional backbone of the family. Later, Michael Gray, her rediscovered son, enters the clan with his own ambitions.

Each member brings their unique dynamic. Arthur embodies brute force and blind loyalty, but his mental instability makes him unpredictable. John represents the balance between family life and crime until his tragic death. Ada refuses the criminal empire but remains bound by blood. Polly possesses the intelligence and intuition that complement Tommy's tactical genius. Michael, ambitious and educated, progressively becomes a rival rather than an ally. Finn, raised in crime, symbolizes the next generation.

Family dynamics constantly oscillate between absolute loyalty and potential betrayal. Tommy and Arthur share a bond forged in the trenches, but Tommy often manipulates his brother to serve his plans. The relationship with Polly combines mutual respect and conflicts over the family's moral direction. Ada represents the conscience that Tommy systematically ignores. Michael's arrival introduces a generational and ideological fracture that will culminate in open confrontation.

Allies and enemies include Alfie Solomons, the unpredictable Jewish gang leader whose alliance with Tommy defies all logic; Aberama Gold, the gypsy king who seals his alliance through marriage; and various corrupt politicians. Enemies are legion: Billy Kimber and his Birmingham gang, Inspector Campbell and the police, the Italian Changretta mafia, Luca Changretta personally, fascist Oswald Mosley, and ultimately Michael himself. Every alliance is temporary, every enemy potentially deadly.

Iconic Locations

The Garrison Pub is much more than a simple bar: it's the beating heart of the Shelby operation. Located on Garrison Lane, this establishment serves as a meeting room, informal tribunal, and celebration venue. The private snugs at the back host the most delicate negotiations. The sober decoration, the flowing Irish whiskey, and the smoky atmosphere create the perfect ambiance for criminal business. Every glass raised at the Garrison seals a pact or buries an enemy.

Shelby Company Limited represents Tommy's ambition for legitimacy. These offices transform a criminal operation into an apparently respectable business. Paneled walls, competent secretaries, and carefully maintained records create the illusion of normality. Yet behind every legal transaction hides a criminal arrangement. It's here that Tommy orchestrates his boldest plans, transforming dirty money into clean investments.

Arrow House symbolizes the Shelbys' social ascension. This massive country mansion, with its stables, French gardens, and sumptuous salons, represents everything Tommy wanted to become. Every room breathes luxury and power. Yet the atmosphere remains cold, almost mausoleum-like. The ghosts of Tommy's past haunt these opulent corridors. Arrow House becomes as much a gilded prison as a palace, isolating Tommy from the world he sought to conquer.

Racetracks and racing grounds constitute the Shelbys' original playground. This is where it all begins, with race-fixing and illegal betting. From Birmingham's muddy fields to Epsom's prestigious racetracks, these places represent the gang's evolution. The horses themselves become recurring metaphors: freedom, nobility, but also vulnerability. Every fixed race finances expansion, every legitimate victory reinforces the facade of respectability that Tommy meticulously constructs.

Codes & Traditions

The rules of the underworld in 1920s Birmingham follow strict tribal logic. Loyalty to family comes before everything. Betrayals are paid in blood. Verbal agreements have the force of law; breaking one's word is equivalent to signing a death warrant. Respect is obtained through force and intelligence. Territories are sacred; crossing them without permission triggers war. These unwritten codes govern every interaction, every negotiation, every conflict in this parallel world.

The betting business constitutes the foundation of the Shelby empire. In an era when horse racing is the national obsession and illegal betting flourishes, controlling bookmakers means controlling money. The system operates through a network of collectors, messengers, and enforcers. The Shelbys don't just take bets: they fix races, corrupt jockeys, and manipulate odds. This apparently simple business generates colossal fortunes.

Gang wars follow their own rituals. Warnings often precede violence: an encrypted message, a symbolic corpse, a show of force. Confrontations unfold according to tacit rules: no women or children, no police. Vendettas can last generations, each death calling for a response. The Shelbys excel in this organized violence, transforming each conflict into an opportunity for expansion. Brutality meets strategy in a precarious balance.

Social ascension represents the ultimate but most difficult goal to achieve. Moving from gangster to gentleman requires more than money: one must erase the past, acquire manners, establish connections. Tommy uses several strategies: investing in legitimate businesses, frequenting the right people, entering politics, marrying into high society. Each step meets resistance and contempt from the established aristocracy. The Shelbys' ascension illustrates British class struggle, where origin remains an indelible mark despite acquired wealth.
